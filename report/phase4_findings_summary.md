# Phase 4 Findings Summary — Illustrative Scenario Analysis

## Objective

Phase 4 converts Phase 3 semiconductor-linked revenue exposure into scenario-based impact estimates.

The model applies disruption severity factors to the base-case Revenue at Risk from Phase 3:

```text
Scenario Impact = Base-Case Revenue at Risk × Disruption Severity
```

Important: the scenario severities are **not probabilities** and are **not direct historical measurements of Taiwan semiconductor supply loss**. They are analyst-defined stress assumptions informed by historical semiconductor shortage impacts and Taiwan foundry disruption risk literature.

## Revised Scenario Definitions

| Scenario | Severity | Interpretation | Rationale |
|---|---:|---|---|
| Mild | 10% | Short-term allocation issue, delivery delay, or limited capacity constraint | Conservative stress case broadly consistent with documented 2021 chip shortage impacts, including Ford CFO guidance that chip-supplier estimates could imply losing 10–20% of planned Q1 production and IHS Markit estimating nearly 700,000 fewer global vehicles in the quarter. |
| Moderate | 20% | Partial disruption to Taiwan-related semiconductor inputs affecting a meaningful share of semiconductor-linked exposure | Elevated stress case near the upper end of the documented Ford 10–20% planned-production impact range during the 2021 chip shortage. |
| Severe | 40% | Major disruption short of full Taiwanese foundry shutdown | High-impact stress case informed by severe firm-level shortage episodes such as Toyota’s reported 40% September 2021 global production cut, while remaining below the extreme full-shutdown case discussed in SIA/BCG literature. |

The previous 60% severe case was replaced with a **40% severe case** to make the scenario framework more conservative and easier to defend using publicly documented historical disruption benchmarks.

## Base-Case Scenario Results

| Industry | Revenue at Risk (USD bn) | Mild 10% | Moderate 20% | Severe 40% |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Electronics | 5.77 | 0.58 | 1.15 | 2.31 |
| Automotive | 0.92 | 0.09 | 0.18 | 0.37 |
| HDD / Storage | 0.87 | 0.09 | 0.17 | 0.35 |
| Home Appliances | 0.78 | 0.08 | 0.16 | 0.31 |

|---|---:|
| Mild | 0.83 |
| Moderate | 1.67 |

## Main Interpretation

Electronics remains the largest exposure across all scenarios because its base-case semiconductor-linked revenue exposure is much larger than the other industries. Under the Moderate 20% scenario, electronics alone accounts for approximately USD 1.15 billion of estimated semiconductor-linked impact. Under the Severe 40% scenario, electronics accounts for approximately USD 2.31 billion.

Automotive, HDD/storage, and home appliances show lower absolute exposure values. However, automotive and HDD/storage remain strategically important because semiconductor shortages can create production bottlenecks: a shortage of relatively low-cost chips can delay or stop production of high-value final goods.

## Methodological Limitation

This is a scenario-based exposure model, not a probability-weighted expected loss model. The results should not be interpreted as actual forecast losses.

The 10%, 20%, and 40% severity levels are analyst-defined stress assumptions. They are informed by historical semiconductor shortage impacts but are not direct measurements of Taiwan semiconductor supply reduction. The model assumes that the Phase 3 revenue-at-risk estimates represent the semiconductor-linked exposure base, and scenario severity represents the share of that exposure affected under each disruption case.

A more advanced version would require firm-level bill-of-materials data, inventory days, supplier-specific sourcing, substitution options, and probability estimates for each disruption event.
